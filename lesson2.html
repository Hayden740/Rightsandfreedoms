The Freedom Rides

The SAFA bus outside Hotel Boggabilla, where students rested before interviewing people at the Aboriginal station.
Protesters holding a "Student Action for Aborigines" banner during the 1965 Freedom Ride.

The Freedom Riders were 30 students who formed the group S.A.F.A (Student Action for Aborigines) to advocate for the rights of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples.

The group was created in 1964 when students at the University of Sydney were challenged by onlookers at a protest against American racial segregation to "look at" and "do something" about Australia's own Aboriginal rights situation.

'Student Action for Aborigines (S.A.F.A) stemmed primarily from the demonstration carried out in mid-1964 by Sydney University Students against America's treatment of the race issue. Onlookers at the demonstration challenged the students to "look at" and "do something" about Australia's Aboriginal question firstly. It brought home to students the fact that they must get their own house in order before they queried someone else's.' — Charles Perkins, Annual Report of SAFA to the 8th Annual Conference on Aboriginal Affairs, April 1965

Notable members included Charles Perkins (a prominent Aboriginal activist and later influential government bureaucrat), Ann Curthoys (historian and author), Jim Spigelman (later Chief Justice of NSW), and Darce Cassidy (ABC News reporter).

Freedom Rides in America

A Freedom Riders bus burning outside Anniston, Alabama, May 14, 1961

On May 14, 1961, a group of thirteen African American and white civil rights activists launched the Freedom Rides — a series of bus trips through the American South to protest segregation in interstate bus terminals. They were recruited by the Congress of Racial Equality (CORE).

African American Freedom Riders attempted to use "whites-only" restrooms and lunch counters, while white Freedom Riders attempted to use "blacks-only" facilities. The Freedom Riders encountered tremendous violence from white protestors, including having their bus bombed outside Anniston, Alabama. 600 federal marshals were eventually sent to stop the violence.

The Wave Hill Walk-Off

Gough Whitlam pours a handful of earth into Vincent Lingiari's hand, symbolically returning Gurindji land, 1975.

On 23 August 1966, 200 Gurindji stockmen, domestic workers and their families initiated strike action at Wave Hill station in the Northern Territory. Negotiations with the station owners, the international food company Vestey Brothers, broke down, leading to a seven-year dispute.

This eventually led to the return of a portion of their homelands to the Gurindji people in 1974, and the passing of the first legislation allowing Indigenous people to claim land title if they could prove a traditional relationship to the country.

"I bin thinkin' this bin Gurindji country. We bin here longa time before them Vestey mob." — Vincent Lingiari, 1966
