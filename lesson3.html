The 1967 Referendum

After 10 long years of campaigning and gaining public support, the Australian Government finally allowed the public to vote on Aboriginal people 'becoming' Australian citizens. By this time, most white Australians supported Aboriginal people being citizens and getting the right to vote.

Referendum: A general vote by the electorate on a single political question which has been referred to them for a direct decision.

Over 90% of Australians voted YES! This was one of the highest YES votes in Australian referendum history. After the vote, Aboriginal people were now counted in the national Census and the Federal Government gained the power to make laws specifically for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples.

However, First Nations Peoples still had to fight for equal rights to social services such as disability support pensions, unemployment benefits, and old age pensions — all of which white Australians already had access to.

Land Rights

Even after the 1967 Referendum, First Nations peoples continued to fight for recognition of their land — land that had been taken from them without treaty, without consent, and without compensation.

The Aboriginal Embassy tent set up on the lawn of Parliament House, Canberra, 1972
Land Rights protesters at a rally
"The British settlers took our land. No treaties were signed with the tribes. Today we are REFUGEES. Refugees in the country of our ancestors. We live in REFUGEE CAMPS — without land, without employment, without justice." — "Gwalwa Daraniki! This is Our Land!" — Aboriginal land rights document

The Mabo Decision

Eddie Mabo — the man who challenged Australia's legal system for land rights

The Mabo decision was a landmark legal case held in 1992 (Mabo and others v Queensland No. 2). On 3 June 1992, the High Court of Australia decided that terra nullius (the legal fiction that Australia was "empty land" before European arrival) should not have been applied to Australia.

This decision recognised that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have rights to the land — rights that existed before the British arrived and can still exist today. It led to the passing of the Native Title Act 1993.

Sadly, Eddie Mabo never found out the result of his legal case. He died in January 1992, just five months before the High Court made its decision.

Remember to use a combination of:

Pathos

Focuses on the values and beliefs of the audience. Appeals to empathy, often through personal stories.

Ethos

Focuses on the writer's trustworthiness — "appeal to character" or "appeal to credibility."

Logos

Often called a "logical appeal" — uses facts, statistics and evidence to support the argument.
